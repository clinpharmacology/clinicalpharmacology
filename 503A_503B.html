Section 503A and 503B Compounding Pharmacies

What Are 503A and 503B?

These terms refer to two distinct sections of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FD&C Act) that describe how pharmacies compound medications in the United States.

üíä Section 503A ‚Äî Traditional Compounding Pharmacies

Who they are: Local or community pharmacies that compound customized drugs for individual patients based on a valid prescription.

  • Each compounded product must be patient-specific.
  • Regulated mainly by state pharmacy boards.
  • Not required to follow full FDA manufacturing standards (like cGMP).
  • Cannot make or sell bulk drugs for general use.
Example: A pharmacist compounds a dye-free tablet for a patient allergic to coloring agents.

üíä Section 503B ‚Äî Outsourcing Facilities

Who they are: Larger compounding facilities that produce sterile drugs in bulk without patient-specific prescriptions.

  • Must register with the FDA as outsourcing facilities.
  • Must comply with current Good Manufacturing Practices (cGMP).
  • Can compound sterile drugs in batches for hospital or office use.
  • Subject to FDA inspections and federal oversight.
In short: 503A pharmacies focus on individualized therapy; 503B facilities operate like small-scale manufacturers ensuring sterility and quality under cGMP.

  • 503A: Patient-specific, no FDA approval, state-regulated, not cGMP-compliant.
  • 503B: Bulk sterile production, FDA oversight, cGMP-compliant, but not FDA-approved for efficacy.
  • FDA-Approved: Fully reviewed through NDA/ANDA, supported by clinical and bioequivalence data.

üè≠ Is a 503B Facility Like a Small Drug Factory?

Yes. It operates under cGMP and FDA inspection like a manufacturer, but it does not develop or seek approval for new drugs. Its role is to compound existing APIs into usable sterile preparations.

Most 503B facilities are independent, though some hospitals may own affiliated outsourcing facilities to supply sterile injectables internally.

üß† Clinical Pharmacology Relevance

503B products do not undergo bioequivalence or clinical trials; hence, their PK/PD may differ from approved drugs. However, they ensure continuity of supply in critical settings.

Key takeaway: 503B drugs are sterile and GMP-compliant but not clinically validated for efficacy. FDA-approved drugs are fully evaluated for safety, efficacy, and bioequivalence.
