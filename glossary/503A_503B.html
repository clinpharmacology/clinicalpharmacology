Overview

These refer to sections of the FD&C Act describing two categories of compounding pharmacies:

  • 503A – Traditional Compounding Pharmacies: Local/community pharmacies that compound drugs for individual patients based on prescriptions. Regulated by state boards; patient-specific; not for bulk or resale.
  • 503B – Outsourcing Facilities: Larger facilities producing sterile drugs in bulk without individual prescriptions. Must register with FDA, follow cGMP, and undergo inspections.

Key Differences from FDA-approved Drugs

  • FDA-approved drugs: NDA/ANDA required, full clinical trials for safety & efficacy, commercially mass-produced.
  • 503B compounded drugs: No NDA/ANDA, cGMP compliance, sterility & potency testing, used for shortages or hospital-specific needs.

Clinical Pharmacology Relevance

  • 503A: Individualized therapy; may affect PK/PD due to excipient changes.
  • 503B: Quality-controlled; batch testing reduces variability but efficacy not formally proven.
  • Clinical trials require GMP-compliant drugs; 503B may be used under sponsor oversight, 503A cannot.

Risks and Safety

  • 503B drugs can cause adverse events: contamination, dosing errors, excipient incompatibility, allergic reactions.
  • Adverse events must be reported via FDA MedWatch.
  • Historical incidents (e.g., NECC 2012) led to 503B regulation under the Drug Quality and Security Act 2013.
